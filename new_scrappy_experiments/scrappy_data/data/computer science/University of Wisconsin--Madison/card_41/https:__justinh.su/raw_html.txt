I was a graduate student in the <a href=\"https://cis.upenn.edu\">Department of Computer Science</a> at the <a href=\"https://www.upenn.edu\">University of Pennsylvania</a>.</p>\n<section id=\"research-interests\" class=\"level2\">\n<h2>Research Interests</h2>\n<p>I design methods to <strong>formally verify</strong> that programs are correct, especially programs that use <strong>randomization</strong>. Such programs can be easy to show correct on paper, but surprisingly challenging for computers to analyze. Accordingly, my research blends ideas from two classical areas of computer science: <strong>randomized algorithms</strong> from theoretical computer science (<strong>TCS</strong>) and <strong>formal verification</strong>.</p>\n<p>Drawing inspiration from how humans reason about randomized algorithms, we can build simpler and more automated verification techniques. In the past, I\u2019ve applied this approach to properties like <strong>accuracy</strong>, <strong>incentive compatibility</strong>, Markov chain <strong>mixing</strong>, and various notions of <strong>algorithmic stability</strong>.</p>\n<p>A particular focus of my work has been <a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_privacy\"><strong>differential privacy</strong></a>, a rigorous definition of privacy that is currently under extensive study. I have investigated a variety of formal methods\u2014such as <a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_system\"><strong>type systems</strong></a> and <a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hoare_logic\"><strong>program logics</strong></a>\u2014to verify that programs are differentially private.</p>\n<p>From a more traditional algorithms perspective, I am also interested in applying differential privacy to optimization, machine learning, and mechanism design.</p>\n</section>\n<section id=\"teaching\" class=\"level2\">\n<h2>Teaching</h2>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Introduction to the Theory and Design of PL (CS 538)</strong>: <a href=\"https://pages.cs.wisc.edu/~justhsu/teaching/current/cs538/\">S20</a></li>\n<li><strong>Security and Privacy in Data Science (CS 763)</strong>: <a href=\"https://pages.cs.wisc.edu/~justhsu/teaching/current/cs763/\">F19</a></li>\n<li><strong>Introduction to the Theory and Design of PL (CS 538)</strong>: <a href=\"https://pages.cs.wisc.edu/~justhsu/teaching/s19/cs538/\">S19</a></li>\n<li><strong>Topics in Security and Privacy Technologies (CS 839)</strong>: <a href=\"https://pages.cs.wisc.edu/~justhsu/teaching/f18/cs839/\">F18</a></li>\n</ul>\n</section>\n<section id=\"service\" class=\"level2\">\n<h2>Service</h2>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>2021</strong> ESOP, PLMW@POPL</li>\n<li><strong>2020</strong> AAAI, CSF, LAFI, WoLLIC, PLMW@POPL</li>\n<li><strong>2019</strong> POPL, PLMW@POPL, POST, CSF, DARS (co-chair)</li>\n<li><strong>2018</strong> LICS, WWW</li>\n<li><strong>2017</strong> FCS, TPDP, MFPS</li>\n<li><strong>2016</strong> PLDI (ERC)</li>\n</ul>\n</section>\n    </div>\n  </div>\n\n  <div id=\"card\">\n    <div id=\"mini-header-alt\">\n      News\n    </div>\n    <ul>\n<li><strong>01/2020</strong> This spring, I\u2019m teaching an undergraduate course <strong>Introduction to the Theory and Design of Programming Languages (CS 538)</strong>, covering Haskell and Rust.
