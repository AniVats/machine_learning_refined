Complexity.\n\n</p><p>\n\n</p><h3>Biography</h3>\n\n<p>\n\nDieter van Melkebeek is a Professor of Computer Sciences. \nHe completed his Ph.D. in 1999 at the University of Chicago, and \nwas a postdoc at DIMACS and the Institute for Advanced Study (Princeton) \nbefore joining the faculty at the University of Wisconsin-Madison. \nHe has held visiting appointments at \nCWI (Amsterdam), \nENS (Paris),\nthe Fields Institute (Toronto), \nHumboldt University (Berlin), \nMSRI (Berkeley), \nthe Simons Institute (Berkeley), \nUPC (Barcelona), and\nthe Weizmann Institute of Science (Rehovot). \n\n</p><p>\n\nProfessor Van Melkebeek's research interests lie in \ncomputational complexity theory and \nthe theory of computing. \nHe has developed lower bounds for the resources needed to solve \nNP-complete problems, and\nstudies the power of randomness in computation. \nHe earned the 1999 ACM Doctoral Dissertation Award and an NSF CAREER award\nfor his research.\n\n</p><p>\n\nProfessor Van Melkebeek teaches \nalgorithms and computational complexity theory \nat the undergraduate and graduate level. \nHe is also the longtime coach for the UW-Madison teams participating in \nACM's International Collegiate Programming Contest.\n\n</p><p>\n\nProfessor Van Melkebeek instituted the Computational Complexity Foundation,\nand chaired the program and steering committees of the Conference on \nComputational Complexity, and the ACM Doctoral Dissertation Award committee.\nHe serves or has served on the editorials boards of \nthe Leibniz International Proceedings in Informatics (LIPIcs),\nSIAM Journal on Computing, \nComputational Complexity, \nACM Transactions on Computation Theory, and\nthe Electronic Colloquium on Computational Complexity.\n\n<!-- END of body of the web
