My work focuses on the interface between\ncomputers and the visual and tangible world:  acquisition, representation,\nanalysis, and fabrication of 3D shape, motion, surface appearance, and\nscattering.  I am interested in topics including computational fabrication;\n3D scan acquisition, registration, and reconstruction; learning for\nlow-level image and shape analysis; robotic localization and planning; and\nBRDF capture and representation.\n<!--\nalgorithms for processing geometry and reflectance, including registration,\nmatching, completion, hierarchical decomposition, symmetry analysis,\nsampling, and depiction.\n-->\nApplications of this work include documentation of cultural heritage\nartifacts and sites, appearance and performance capture for digital humans,\nand illustrative depiction through line drawings and non-photorealistic\nshading models.\n<!--\n<p>\nHere are some of my recent projects:\n<ul>\n\t<li> <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/proj/thera/\"><b>Reassembling the Thera Frescoes.</b></a>\n\tWe are engaged in a project to assist archaeologists and\n\tconservators in reconstructing frescoes excavated from the\n\tlate-bronze-age settlement of Akrotiri, on the Aegean\n\tisland of Thera (modern-day Santorini).  The project\n\tincorporates systems for digitization of high-quality 3D\n\tgeometry, normal maps, and color, as well as computer-aided\n\tmatching of fragments on the basis of shape (3D edge\n\tprofile), thickness, color, surface impressions,\n\tprovenance, and other cues.  This project is ongoing (the\n\tproject page is <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/proj/thera/\">here</a>), and\n\tan initial publication on the digitization and matching\n\tsystems is <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Brown_2008_ASF/index.php\">here</a>.\n\n\t<li> <b>Rendering with multi-light image collections and images\n\twith normals.</b>  We are investigating acquisition, analysis,\n\tand rendering techniques that begin with datasets inherently\n\tmore powerful than single images, yet easier to acquire with\n\thigh quality than full 3D scans.  The\n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Fattal_2007_MSA/index.php\">Multiscale\n\tShape and Detail Enhancement</a> paper begins with just a few\n\timages of an object under varying lighting (a Multi-Light\n\tImage Collection or \"MLIC\"), and produces a\n\tnon-photorealistic high-detail illustation by performing\n\tlocal statistical analyses at multiple scales.  The\n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Sunkavalli_2007_FTV/index.php\">Factored\n\tTime-Lapse Video</a> paper attempts to separate the contributions\n\tof shadowing, reflectance, sunlight, and skylight from an\n\tuncalibrated full-day time-lapse video of an exterior scene.\n\n\t<p>\n\tI am also very interested in the power of images with a per-pixel\n\tcolor and normal (\"RGBN\" images), and our recent\n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Toler-Franklin_2007_IOC/index.php\">RGBN NPR</a>\n\tpaper shows how to adapt signal processing, segmentation, and\n\tstylized depiction algorithms to this new datatype.  Earlier\n\twork with these datasets included a paper on\n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Nehab_2005_ECP/index.php\">combining normal maps\n\twith scanned 3D data</a> to produce high-quality high-resolution\n\t3D models.\n\n\t<li> <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/proj/3dscanning/\"><b>New techniques for range scanning.</b></a>\n\tAlthough 3D scanning has become a widely-used technique, most\n\tcommercially available systems acquire data at relatively low rates\n\tand require careful control over the setup and calibration of\n\tcameras and/or active light sources.  I am interested in techniques\n\tfor making range scanning systems faster, more flexible, more\n\trobust, and easier to use.  <a href=\"papers/rt_model/\">One\n\tsystem</a> I worked on lets you take an object, wave it around by\n\thand in front of the scanner, and get a full 3D model of the\n\tobject, while being able to see the model <i>as it is being\n\tconstructed</i>.  This combines a\n\t<a href=\"papers/realtimerange/\">real-time structured-light\n\trangefinder</a> with algorithms for <a href=\"papers/fasticp/\">fast\n\tregistration</a>, merging, and display.\n\n\t<p>\n\tI am also interested in \"filling in the gaps\" between traditionally\n\tseparate methods for acquisition of 3D shape.  For example,\n\tour <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Davis_2005_SS:/index.php\">spacetime stereo</a>\n\tpaper argues that methods such as structured light and traditional\n\tstereo may be considered points on a continuum of techniques that\n\tobtain depth via triangulation from 2 viewpoints (that may contain\n\tcameras or light sources), and that obtain correspondences over\n\twindows in space, time, or both.  This suggests several hybrid\n\tmethods, such as temporal stereo, that achieve high accuracy while\n\tincreasing ease of use by not requiring camera-to-projector\n\tsynchronization and calibration.  More recent work has focused on \n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Young_2007_VSL/index.php\">taking advantage of\n\tmultiview coding constraints</a> to produce a multi-camera system\n\tthat is <i>guaranteed</i> to not have matching ambiguity.\n\n\t<p>\n\tMy interests in range scanning began with my work on\n\t<a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/projects/mich/\">Digital\n\tMichelangelo</a>, which was an epic effort to acquire\n\thigh-resolution three-dimensional models of some well-known sculptures.\n\tAs part of this project, I spent nine months in Italy doing things like\n\t<a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/projects/mich/images/packingup_small.jpg\">heavy lifting</a>,\n\tdebugging hardware and software, <a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/projects/mich/mgantry-in-lab//mgantry-truss-extensions-s.jpg\">heavy\n\tlifting</a>, doing some actual scanning, and <a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/projects/mich/medici/view-of-tower-s.jpg\">heavy\n\tlifting</a>.  My particular specialty was the <a href=\"research/dmichcolorpipeline/\">color\n\tprocessing pipeline</a> (some results are <a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/projects/mich/matthew/matthew.html\">here</a>\n\tand <a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/projects/mich/color-david/color-david.html\">here</a>).\n\tI also worked on the <a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/projects/mich/kiosk/kiosk.html\">kiosk</a>\n\tthat displays our 3D models in the Galleria dell'Accademia, right next\n\tto the <a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/projects/mich/david/david.html\">big guy</a> himself.\n\n\t<li> <b>Shape analysis, registration, and matching.</b>\n\t3D scans obtained from a rangescanner typically must be aligned\n\tto each other before a full model may be reconstructed.  I am\n\tinterested in both <a href=\"papers/fasticp/\">rigid-body</a>\n\tregistration (including <a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/papers/stabicp/\">stability\n\tanalysis</a>) and <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Brown_2004_NRA/index.php\">non-rigid methods</a>,\n\tthe latter being especially applicable when scanner miscalibration has\n\tresulted in warped scans.  Using this technique we are finally making\n\tinroads into <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Brown_2007_GNA/index.php\">producing\n\thigh-quality versions of the Digital Michelangelo scans</a>.\n\tTogether with Benedict Brown and Misha Kazhdan, we taught a <a\n\thref=\"/~bjbrown/iccv05_course/\">course on \"3D Scan Matching and\n\tRegistration\"</a> at ICCV 2005.\n\t\n\t<p> I have also worked on shape analysis projects such as <a\n\thref=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Rusinkiewicz_2004_ECA/index.php\">curvature estimation</a>, <a\n\thref=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/proj/shape/\">shape matching and retrieval</a>, and\n\tdetection of perfect and imperfect symmetries, both <a\n\thref=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Kazhdan_2004_SDA/index.php\">through the center of\n\tmass</a> and <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Podolak_2006_APS/index.php\">through\n\tarbitrary planes</a>.  Using these detected symmetries, we can\n\tperform <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Podolak_2007_SRO/index.php\">remeshing</a>\n\tthat yields attractive low-polygon-count models\n\tthat preserve and strengthen the existing symmetries.\n\n\t<li> <b>Shape depiction, including\n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/proj/sugcon/\">line drawings with suggestive contours.</a></b>\n\tLine drawings made from 3D models usually contain strokes placed at\n\tthe contours (or silhouettes - locations of depth discontinuities)\n\tand at creases or sharp bends on the surface.  <i>Suggestive\n\tcontours</i> are a different family of lines that complement\n\tcontours and add a significant amount of visual information that\n\thelps dramatically in the perception of shape.  The SIGGRAPH 2003\n\tpaper on suggestive contours is\n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/DeCarlo_2003_SCF/index.php\">here</a>. \n\tOur <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/DeCarlo_2004_IRO/index.php\">NPAR\n\t2004 paper</a> focuses on analyzing the stability of\n\tsuggestive contours under changes in viewpoint and on fast\n\textraction from triangular meshes, while our\n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Burns_2005_LDF/index.php\">SIGGRAPH 2005 paper</a>\n\tlooks at suggestive contours in volumetric data.\n\tSample code for rendering suggestive contours\n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/proj/sugcon/index.html#software\">is available</a>,\n\tand check out the\n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/proj/sugcon/models/index.html\">suggestive contour gallery</a>.\n\tDoug DeCarlo, Adam Finkelstein, and I also taught a course on\n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/proj/sg05lines/\">\"Line Drawings from 3D Models\"</a>\n\tat SIGGRAPH 2005.\n\n\t<p>\n\tA recent project involves \"exaggerated shading\", which adjusts\n\tthe effective light position for different areas of the surface.\n\tIt reveals detail regardless of surface orientation and, by\n\toperating at multiple scales, is designed to convey detail at\n\tall frequencies simultaneously.  The SIGGRAPH 2006 paper is\n\t<a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Rusinkiewicz_2006_ESF/index.php\">here</a>,\n\tand <a href= \"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/proj/xshade/\">source code</a> is also available.\n\n\t<li> <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/proj/appear/\"><b>Measurement and representation\n\tof realistic surface appearance.</b></a>\n\tThe appearance of a surface can be characterized by the amount of\n\tlight it reflects at each surface position, for each pair of\n\tdirections of incident and reflected light. (For translucent\n\tmaterials, the function must also include the position at which\n\tthe light leaves the surface.) \tThis is a complete\n\tdescription of an object's interaction with light, and embodies\n\tenough information to reconstruct its appearance in any\n\tenvironment.  The difficulties with capturing and representing this\n\treflectance are many: the datasets are large (gigabytes for even a\n\tsparse sampling of the spatially-varying reflectance), their\n\tunderlying structure is often only exposed through complex\n\treparameterization, and it is difficult to compress them while both\n\tretaining visual fidelity and maintaining the ability to use the\n\tdata with algorithms for photorealistic lighting simulation.\n\n\t<p>\n\tWe have recently been focusing on <i>factored</i> reflectance\n\trepresentations, in which the high-dimensional reflectance function\n\tis first <a href=\"papers/brdf_change_of_variables/\">reparameterized</a>\n\tusing a change-of-basis transform that exposes its underlying\n\tstructure, then is factored into low-dimensional pieces.  Our <a\n\thref=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Lawrence_0000_EBI/index.php\">SIGGRAPH 2004\n\tpaper</a> focuses on a factorization specially designed to make\n\timportance sampling (in the context of a photorealistic global\n\tillumination system) efficient.  More recently, we have focused\n\ton a complete hierarchical <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Lawrence_2006_IST/index.php\">\"Inverse\n\tShade Tree\"</a> decomposition of spatially-varying reflectance,\n\tin which a material's appearance is broken down into intuitive,\n\t<i>editable</i> lower-dimensional components.  Together with\n\tresearchers at KU Leuven and MERL, we have also developed methods for\n\tdecomposing <a href=\"//gfx.cs.princeton.edu/pubs/Peers_2006_ACF/index.php\">heterogeneous\n\tsubsurface scattering</a> in materials such as veined marble.\n\n\t<p>\n\tMy previous work related to appearance includes helping design the\n\t<a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/projects/gantry/\">Stanford\n\tspherical gantry</a>, a computer-controlled, multi-axis measurement\n\tapparatus for measuring surface geometry and reflectance, and\n\tthe <a href=\"software/bv/\"><tt>bv</tt></a> program for displaying\n\ta variety of analytic BRDF functions.\n\n\t<li> <a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/software/qsplat/\"><b>QSplat.</b></a>\n\tOne of the results of the Digital Michelangelo Project was a series\n\tof meshes ranging from 100 million to 1 billion points.  This is\n\tsignificantly more than can be displayed interactively on current\n\thardware.  QSplat is a rendering system that combines a\n\tmultiresolution representation based on a bounding sphere hierarchy\n\twith splat rendering, resulting in a system capable of rendering\n\thuge scanned meshes at interactive frame rates.  The QSplat\n\tsoftware is <a href=\"software/qsplat/\">publically available</a>,\n\tand a description of the algorithm may be found in a SIGGRAPH 2000\n\t<a href=\"papers/qsplat/\">paper</a>.  It is also possible to make\n\tQSplat <a href=\"papers/sqsplat/\">stream data across the net</a>.\n\n\t<li> <b>Light field acquisition</b>.\n\tA <a href=\"//graphics.stanford.edu/projects/lightfield/\">lightfield</a>\n\tis an image-based representation of light flow through a region of\n\tspace.
