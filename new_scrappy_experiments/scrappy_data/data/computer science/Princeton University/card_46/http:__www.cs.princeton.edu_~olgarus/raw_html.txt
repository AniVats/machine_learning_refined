Pelletier.&nbsp;It makes a really solid point that while <em>mental stress</em> may be helpful for motivation,&nbsp;<em>physical stress</em> (heart pounding, muscles tightening, sinking feeling in your stomach) is strictly counter-productive on every level -- except when you're running from an actual physical tiger, which you probably aren't. So the book describes some straight-forward&nbsp;techniques to trick your body into being less physically stressed. </li>\n<li>A 2015 study&nbsp;<a target=\"_blank\" href=\"http://science.sciencemag.org/content/347/6219/262\">published in Science</a>&nbsp;and <a target=\"_blank\" href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/speaking-of-science/wp/2015/01/15/gender-gap-women-welcome-in-hard-working-fields-but-genius-fields-are-male-dominated-study-finds/\">extensively covered by&nbsp;the media</a> led by <a target=\"_blank\" href=\"http://www.princeton.edu/~sjleslie/\">Sarah-Jane Leslie</a> found that scientific fields where <q>innate brilliance</q> is <em>believed</em> to be required tend to attract fewer women and racial minorities. This is an incredibly important but deeply distressing finding. It's particularly frustrating since <q>innate brilliance</q> is not even really a thing. For example, check out <a target=\"_blank\" href=\"http://www.amazon.com/Peak-Secrets-New-Science-Expertise\">Peak: Secrets from the New Science of Expertise</a>&nbsp;by Anders Ericsson which essentially invalidates this concept entirely.
