Data systems have not\n        adapted to address these demands effectively: databases' specialized\n        query languages and structure create barriers for non-experts, while\n        the lack of native support for important computing needs leaves\n        experts to develop application-specific solutions themselves. Our work\n        removes data-use barriers by simplifying access for non-experts to\n        data and by augmenting database functionality with advanced\n        problem-solving capabilities, thus simplifying analytics workflows by\n        moving them closer to the data.\n        </p>\n\n<table class=\"projects\">\n    <tbody><tr>\n        <td class=\"projectleft\"></td>\n        <td class=\"projecticon\">\n        <a href=\"http://packagebuilder.cs.umass.edu/\" title=\"Package queries\">\n            <img src=\"images/package.png\" class=\"projecticon\" alt=\"Package queries\" border=\"0\"></a></td>\n        <td class=\"projectmiddle\"></td>\n        <td class=\"projectdetails\">\n            <b>PackageBuilder: supporting queries for packages</b> <a href=\"http://packagebuilder.cs.umass.edu/\" title=\"Package queries\">[Project page]</a>\n\n            <br>\n            Traditional database queries follow a simple model: they define\n            constraints that each tuple in the result must satisfy. This model\n            is computationally efficient, as the database system can evaluate\n            the query conditions on each tuple individually. Yet, data is\nan imperfect medium, tainted by errors, omissions, and biases. As a result,\ndiscrimination shows up in many data-driven applications, such as\nadvertisements, hotel bookings, image search, and vendor services. Biases in\ndata and software risk forming, propagating, and perpetuating biases in\nsociety. Data management research should develop tools to detect, inform, and\nmitigate the effects of bias, skew, and misuse in data-driven processes.\n        </p>\n\n<table class=\"projects\">\n    <tbody><tr>\n        <td class=\"projectleft\"></td>\n        <td class=\"projecticon\">\n        <a href=\"http://fairness.cs.umass.edu/\" title=\"Fairness testing\">\n            <img src=\"images/fairness.png\" class=\"projecticon\" alt=\"Fairness testing\" border=\"0\"></a></td>\n        <td class=\"projectmiddle\"></td>\n        <td class=\"projectdetails\">\n            <b>Fairness testing</b> <a href=\"http://fairness.cs.umass.edu/\" title=\"Fairness testing\">[Project page]</a>\n\n            <br>\n            Our work studied software fairness and discrimination and produced a\n            testing-based method for measuring if and how much software\n            discriminates, focusing on causality in discriminatory behavior.\n            Our approach, Themis, is the first framework of its kind that\n            automatically generates efficient test suites to measure\n            discrimination. Our techniques rely on reasoning about\n            causal relationships between inputs and outputs of a system.\n            Understanding how inputs affect software behavior can empower\n            developers to control for bias in data and ensure more fair use of\n            software systems.\n<br>\n<b>Publications:</b> <a href=\"https://people.cs.umass.edu/~ameli/projects/fairness/papers/discriminationTesting.pdf\">[ESEC/FSE 2017]</a>\n<a href=\"http://people.cs.umass.edu/brun/pubs/pubs/Angell18fse-demo.pdf\">[ESEC/FSE 2018 demo]</a>\n<a href=\"http://people.cs.umass.edu/brun/pubs/pubs/Brun18fse-nier.pdf\">[ESEC/FSE 2018 vision]</a>\n<br>\n<img src=\"images/award.png\" alt=\"award\" style=\"vertical-align:sub\" width=\"15px\" border=\"0\">\n<b>Awards:</b> ACM SIGSOFT Distinguished Paper Award\n<br>\n      </td>\n    </tr></tbody></table>\n\n    <br>\n    <p></p>\n\n    <table class=\"projects\">\n        <tbody><tr>\n            <td class=\"projectleft\"></td>\n            <td class=\"projecticon\">\n                <img src=\"images/diversity.png\" class=\"projecticon\" alt=\"RC-index\" border=\"0\"></td>\n            <td class=\"projectmiddle\"></td>\n            <td class=\"projectdetails\">\n                <b>Fast diverse data retrieval</b>\n\n                <br>\n                    Data skew is often a cause of algorithmic bias, and the\n                    ability to retrieve balanced, diverse datasets can\n                    mitigate the underlying problem. Data is produced at\n        unprecedented rates, from sources that are broad, varied, and\n        unreliable, and through large-scale processes that introduce their own\n        inaccuracies (e.g., structured data extraction from unstructured\n        text). Traditional data cleaning techniques identify discrepancies and\n        purge datasets of errors, but they treat the manifestation of a\n        problem, not its root cause. They disregard the fact that errors are\n        often systemic, inherent to the process that produces the data, and\n        thus will keep occurring unless the problems are corrected at their\n        source. Our work offers crucial insights into data quality issues:\n        instead of repairing the errors themselves, our research focuses on\n        diagnosing the reasons for the errors and identifying repairs in the\n        processes that produce the data.\n        </p>\n\n        <table class=\"projects\">\n            <tbody><tr>\n                <td class=\"projectleft\"></td>\n                <td class=\"projecticon\">\n                    <img src=\"images/dataxray.png\" class=\"projecticon\" alt=\"Data X-Ray\" border=\"0\"></td>\n                <td class=\"projectmiddle\"></td>\n                <td class=\"projectdetails\">\n                    <b>Data X-Ray: Diagnosing errors in data systems</b><br>\n                     Data X-Ray is a diagnostic framework for profiling errors\n                     in data and determining systemic reasons for them in\n                     internet-scale knowledge extraction pipelines. Even simple transformations through database queries\nobscure the origins of data and the derivation of results. The goal of my\nresearch is to promote users' trust in data and systems through support for\nunderstanding and explanations. Explanations provide opportunities for systems\nto interact with humans and obtain feedback, improving their operation.\nExplanations also allow domain experts and system developers to understand\nsystem decisions and improve system function.\n</p>\n\n\n<table class=\"projects\">\n    <tbody><tr>\n        <td class=\"projectleft\"></td>\n        <td class=\"projecticon\">\n        <a href=\"http://people.cs.umass.edu/~ameli/projects/causality/\" title=\"Causality\">\n            <img src=\"images/causality.png\" class=\"projecticon\" alt=\"Causality\" border=\"0\"></a></td>\n        <td class=\"projectmiddle\"></td>\n        <td class=\"projectdetails\">\n            <b>Causal analysis and explanations in data management</b> <a href=\"http://people.cs.umass.edu/~ameli/projects/causality/\" title=\"Causality\">[Project page (causality)]</a>\n\n            <br>\n            Our research investigates techniques that help users understand the\n            results of their queries by analyzing the history of data\n            transformations (provenance).   settings.\n<br>\n<b>Publications (sample):</b>\n<a href=\"http://www.vldb.org/pvldb/vol12/p779-wang.pdf\">[PVLDB 2019]</a>\n<a href=\"http://sites.computer.org/debull/A18june/p47.pdf\">[DE Bulletin
