data-omni-click=\"r'article',r'',d,r'intext',r'0',r'None'\">the tragedy seen all around the world</a>, flames leaping from the top of Notre-Dame Cathedral, there was a smaller one, thousands of miles data-omni-click=\"r'article',r'',d,r'intext',r'1',r'None'\">died</a> on November 16, 2018, from brain cancer. He was 49. He had dedicated his life to the study of medieval architecture, its mysteries and resonances, blending in his interest in technology to create novel ways of studying centuries-old buildings.</p><div data-template=\"hippo/components/ads/article-house-desktop.html\" data-pos=\"housepromo-d\" id=\"housepromo-d\" class=\" ad-housepromo-d-wrapper\"><gpt-ad targeting-pos=\"housepromo-d1\" class=\"kvr-recirc c-ad c-ad--house is-registered ad-called\" lazy-load=\"2\" id=\"gpt-unit-1\" style=\"display: none;\"></gpt-ad></div><div data-template=\"hippo/components/ads/article-mobile.html\" data-native=\"standard,gift\" data-pos=\"boxinjector-m\" class=\" ad-boxinjector-m-wrapper\"><gpt-ad targeting-pos=\"boxinjector-m1\" targeting-native=\"standard,gift\" class=\"kvr-native kvr-injector c-ad c-ad--article-width s-native s-native--standard s-native--small kvr-native--article-mobile-flex is-registered ad-called ad-loaded ad-loaded--standard\" lazy-load=\"2\" id=\"gpt-unit-2\" data-google-query-id=\"CN-WnJjc-ucCFQOMYgodtOcJlg\"><div id=\"google_ads_iframe_/4624/TheAtlanticOnline/channel_technology_1__container__\" style=\"border: 0pt none; margin: auto; text-align: center;\"><iframe id=\"google_ads_iframe_/4624/TheAtlanticOnline/channel_technology_1\" title=\"3rd party ad content\" name=\"google_ads_iframe_/4624/TheAtlanticOnline/channel_technology_1\" width=\"300\" height=\"250\" scrolling=\"no\" marginwidth=\"0\" marginheight=\"0\" frameborder=\"0\" srcdoc=\"\" sandbox=\"allow-forms allow-pointer-lock allow-popups allow-same-origin allow-scripts allow-top-navigation-by-user-activation allow-presentation\" style=\"border: 0px; vertical-align: bottom;\" data-google-container-id=\"2\" data-load-complete=\"true\"></iframe></div></gpt-ad></div><p>\u201cWhen you\u2019re working on medieval buildings, it\u2019s difficult to have the impression you can say anything new. They\u2019ve been looked at and written about for ages,\u201d <a href=\"https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jAi29udFMKw\" data-omni-click=\"r'article',r'',d,r'intext',r'2',r'None'\">Tallon told a documentary crew in 2015</a>. \u201cSo I\u2019ve been using more sophisticated technology these days to try to get new answers from the buildings.\u201d</p><p>And so it was that in 2010, Tallon, an art professor at Vassar, took <a href=\"https://w3.leica-geosystems.com/downloads123/zz/general/scanstation%20c10/trustories/laser_light_on_gohtic_architecture_tru_en.pdf\" data-omni-click=\"r'article',r'',d,r'intext',r'3',r'None'\">a Leica ScanStation C10</a> to Notre-Dame and, with the assistance of Columbia\u2019s <a href=\"https://www.cs.columbia.edu/~pblaer/\" data-omni-click=\"r'article',r'',d,r'intext',r'4',r'None'\">Paul Blaer</a>, began to painstakingly scan every piece of the structure, inside and out. They mounted the Leica on a tripod, put up markers throughout the space, and set the machine to work. Over five days, they positioned the scanner again and again\u201450 times in all\u2014to create an unmatched record of the reality of one of the world\u2019s most awe-inspiring buildings, represented as a series of points in space. Tallon also took high-resolution panoramic photos to map onto the three-dimensional forms that the laser scanner could create.</p>  </section></div><aside class=\"l-rail l-rail--right l-rail--1\" with the building having sustained untold but very substantial damage, the data that Tallon and Blaer created could be an invaluable aid to whoever is charged with rebuilding the structure. But on the other hand, he knew it so well and had so much information about how it\u2019s constructed, he would have been so helpful in terms of rebuilding it.\u201d</p><p>Laser scans like Tallon and Blaer\u2019s represent a significant development in the restoration of historic buildings. \u201cHaving the scans is incredibly important in any kind of reconstruction,\u201d said Raymond Pepi, the president of Building Conservation Associates, which was the restoration consultant for St. Patrick\u2019s Cathedral in New York City.</p><p>Megan Rispoli is a specialist in restoring religious buildings and ruins; she also worked on the St. Patrick\u2019s project with her previous firm, Murphy Burnham &amp; Buttrick. She said the value of the laser-scanning data is that they capture the buildings as they really are.</p><p>\u201cIt would capture the cathedral in all its true dimension and imperfection,\u201d Rispoli told me. \u201cWhen you draw a building, you tend to make things perfect right angles, and it helps you make sense of the building. So it tends to be stored quite locally, by just a small number of people. Pepi, for example, said that his firm tends to hold on to the laser data for its project partners, primarily because its clients<b> </b>(such as churches) just don\u2019t have the infrastructure to use it.</p><p>The work at Notre-Dame has not yet begun, but it will be a long, long process. Rispoli thought the first step might be emergency shoring for the walls, as the roof has endured so much damage. Then the rebuilding firms will have to assess the integrity of the stone in the rest of the structure. Pepi said that they\u2019d drill holes in the stone to get core samples, and then they\u2019d look at them alongside known good stone from the cathedral to get a sense of \u201cthe physical condition and mineralogical properties of the stone.\u201d</p><div data-template=\"hippo/components/ads/article-mobile.html\" data-native=\"standard,gift\" data-pos=\"boxinjector-m\" class=\" ad-boxinjector-m-wrapper\"><gpt-ad targeting-pos=\"boxinjector-m5\" targeting-native=\"standard,gift\" class=\"kvr-native kvr-injector c-ad c-ad--article-width s-native s-native--standard s-native--small kvr-native--article-mobile-flex is-registered\" lazy-load=\"2\" id=\"gpt-unit-10\"><gpt-sizeset viewport-size=\"[0, 0]\" sizes=\"mobile-wide,teads,native\"></gpt-sizeset><gpt-sizeset viewport-size=\"[976, 0]\" sizes=\"\"></gpt-sizeset></gpt-ad></div><p>But as the cathedral officials and architectural authorities in France and around the world begin to consider what to do, they will come looking for the laser-data files that Tallon created. Blaer estimated that, despite the high resolution of the scans and panoramic photographs, the files would be roughly a terabyte, small enough to fit on a single hard drive, but unlikely to be stored in the cloud. All those data now exist on a single disk, a tiny portal into the past, which is sitting somewhere on Earth. Blaer thought it might be in the hands of Tallon\u2019s students at Vassar.
