a Fellow of the Institute for Creativity, the Arts and Technology and Fellow of the Institute on Gerontology.&nbsp; She holds a doctorate in Psychology from Stanford and a bachelor\u2019s in English and American Literature and Language from Harvard.&nbsp; She spent sixteen years working in industry and industrial research.</p>\n<p>Establishing accomplishments in Computer Science and related fields include her 1986 LISP textbook and her early CSCW (Computer-Supported Collaborative Work) projects, including her analysis of the Xerox PARC Colab project.</p>\n<p>Much of her work focuses on the design of multi-user face-to-face systems, especially those designed to fit into and improve classroom interactions.&nbsp;&nbsp; This interest dovetails with an interest in the design of technologies as background, the notion of zensign (that what is left out can be as important as what is put into a user interface), phenomena of sharing and human-attention.</p>\n<p>She really cares about how people\u2019s images of themselves are shaped by their interactions with technology.</p>\n<p>Here is a link to the <a href=\"http://thirdlab.cs.vt.edu/\">Third Lab website</a>.</p>\n<p>As of Sept. 2015, we have an exciting new project in the area of <a title=\"Integrated Computational Thinking\" href=\"http://thirdlab.cs.vt.edu/education-and-technology/computational-thinking/integrated-computational-thinking/\">Integrated Computational Thinking</a>!&nbsp; Truly exciting is the <a title=\"CHEM+C Project Video\" href=\"https://vimeo.com/144779914\" target=\"_blank\">three minute video</a> which explains the ideas in non-academic terms.</p>\n<p>Here is an interesting thought, from Jim Holt\u2019s \u201cWhy Does the World Exist?\u201d, LiveRight Press, 2012, p. 126:</p>\n<p>\u201cYou can\u2019t give a once and for all definition of what an explanation is,\u201d Deutsch said.&nbsp; \u201cIn fact, important explanatory advances often change the <em>meaning</em> of explanation.&nbsp; My favorite example is the Newtonian-Galilean revolution, which not only brought in new laws of physics, but also altered the very notion of what a physical law is.&nbsp; Previously, laws had been rules stating what happens.&nbsp; Kepler\u2019s laws, for instance, were about how the planets traveled around the sun in elliptical orbits.&nbsp; Newton\u2019s laws were different.&nbsp; They didn\u2019t talk about planets or ellipses.&nbsp; Instead, Newton\u2019s laws were rules that <em>any</em> such system would obey.&nbsp; It\u2019s a different style of explanation, one that hadn\u2019t been thought of before, one that wouldn\u2019t even have been considered an explanation before.&nbsp; The same kind of explanatory revolution happened a couple of hundred years later with Darwin.&nbsp; Previously, when people asked, \u201cWhy does this animal have the shape it does?\u201d they expected that the answer would cite some <em>property</em> of the shape\u2014that it was efficient, that it was favored by God, and so forth.&nbsp; After Darwin, the answer wasn\u2019t about properties of the shape, but about how that shape had come into existence by evolution.&nbsp; Again, it\u2019s a different style of explanation.\u201d</p>\n<p>Here is a link to the slides for a talk she gave in the CS forum in Fall, 2015 called the <a href=\"http://thirdlab.cs.vt.edu/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/TheNextTenYearsDesigningForEquityAndInclusionCSSeminar2015ForPosting.pdf\">The Next Ten Years: Designing For Equity And Inclusion</a>.</p>\n<p>Here\u2019s a link to a <a href=\"http://thirdlab.cs.vt.edu/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/TatarCV2014-2015FAR.pdf\">c.v.
