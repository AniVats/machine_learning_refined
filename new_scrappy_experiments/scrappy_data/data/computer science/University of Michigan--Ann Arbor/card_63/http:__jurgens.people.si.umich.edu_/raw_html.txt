My current research focuses on four central\n                    themes spanning these fields:\n                    \n                    </p><ul style=\"font-size:16px; font-weight:300; line-height: 24px; margin-left: 20px \">\n                      <li><b>Who are the people communicating?</b> Humans create nearly all of the\n                        text we see, yet often who is communicating is overlooked.  I design new\n                        methods for identifying the demographics of communicators in order to understand large\n                        social systems.\n                      </li>\n                      <li><b>How are people related?</b>  There are certain\n                      things you would tell your friends but not your mother.\n                      Yet, when we study peoples' communication pattern, we\n                      often focus on the message rather than the relationship\n                      between them.  I work on learning to recognize how people\n                      are related to one another and what effect this has on\n                      their communication and behavior.\n                      </li>\n                      <li><b>How does our online behavior affect our offline\n                      behavior?</b>  As our lives are increasingly lived online,\n                      what effect does this have in our offline behavior?  I\n                      study how the experiences we have online --both good and\n                      bad-- affect the actions we take, such as exercising,\n                      eating, and traveling.\n                      </li>\n                      <li><b>How can we make online behavior more civil?</b>\n                      Anyone who has scrolled through a few YouTube comments or\n                      read a newspaper article's comment section knows how\n                      quickly conversation can devolve into incivility.  I\n                      research what effect this has on people and how we can not\n                      only detect such behavior but also mitigate it by\n                      improving empathy.\n                      </li>\n                    </ul>\n                  <p></p>\n                  \n                  <p> My long-term research goal combines human and language technologies to create\n                    <i>social understanding</i> that reflects both the content\n                    and people involved in communication.  In all my research, I\n                    strive to improve social equality by representing all people\n                    participating in these social systems.\n                  </p>\n                    \n                </div>\n              </section>\n\n              <section style=\"padding-top: 51px;\">\n                <p class=\"title\" data-section-title=\"\" style=\"left: 479px; height: 52px;\"><a href=\"#panel2\">Prospective Students</a></p>\n                <div class=\"content\" data-section-content=\"\">                  \n                  \n\n                  <h4>PhD students</h4>\n\n                  <p>I admit roughly one PhD student per year. Sometimes students are co-admitted or co-advised, so the number of admits can vary.</p>\n\n                  <p>For <i>prospective</i> PhDs, I especially like students who come with both a\n                  social and a computational perspective. There are no set\n                  criteria, but you're much better off towards admissions if\n                  you've contacted me (or your advisor has) and let me know of\n                  your interests and goals. Make sure to look over these pages\n                  carefully; the match should be pretty strong. A PhD student is\n                  very costly - in time and money - and I select students for my\n                  research group carefully.</p>\n\n                  <p>If you're a <i>current</i> PhD student <i>outside of\n                  SI</i>, I'm open to collaborations.  One of the best things\n                  about SI is the interdisciplinary environment here and I'm\n                  potentially open to hosting students outside SI (but inside\n                  UM) in lab or co-advising on projects where it makes sense.\n                  Regardless, I'd love to hear from you and you're always\n                  welcome to come take my classes</p>\n                  \n                  <h4>Masters</h4>\n\n                  <p>For <i>current</i>masters students, I typically ask that\n                  you take one of my classes and do well in it before starting\n                  on research.  Often the research you do in one of my graduate\n                  courses can continue on through my lab, if you're interested.\n                  If you've already taken classes before that are similar, send\n                  me an email and we can discuss possibilities.  I will\n                  typically ask new masters students to volunteer in the lab for\n                  a time to show commitment before offering any graduate\n                  assistantship.  This is in part because master's students are\n                  often more caught up in coursework (which is a key part of\n                  graduating), but research requires dedication and continued\n                  effort; demonstrating these early and often generally means\n                  funding sooner.</p>\n\n                  <p>For <i>prospective</i>masters students, I do not have any\n                  role in MSI admissions but please feel free to drop me an\n                  email once you've been accepted.  Showing interest in research\n                  prior to starting is usually good sign and gives you more\n                  opportunities during your masters program to see if research\n                  is something you might want to consider.</p>\n\n                  <h4>Undergraduates</h4>\n                  \n                  <p>I enjoy working with undergraduates and generally have a\n                  few students working on various projects in my lab.  I do look\n                  for some serious work and effort associated with your research\n                  project , but in turn, if you want a recommendation letter\n                  later, it will reflect that effort.  Typically, the biggest\n                  issue I face is students who over-estimate their time\n                  availability and disappear as coursework starts to pile up.\n                  Good research takes time and consistent effort; bugs will\n                  happen, experiments will fail, and programs will take time to\n                  run, so being able to dedicate a few hours every day (<b>no\n                  joke!</b>) to research is critical.  If you can't see yourself\n                  doing that, it's best to reach out during a semester you think\n                  you will have more time. </p>\n\n\n                  <p> I have the easiest time working with technically-oriented\n                    students, who already have good programming skills.  You\n                    will typically be paired with a PhD student who can serve as\n                    an extra mentor and answer your questions.  For some\n                    exceptionally strong students, we can try to get you a\n                    project all to yourself, but this usually requires you\n                    having done research prior.  Occasionally, for bigger\n                    projects, I might pair you with another undergrad so you get\n                    to be your own research team.</p>\n\n                  <p> As a undergrad, you will be expected to join our group's\n                  research meetings.  I typically also try to meet undergrads\n                  twice per week for short meetings so I can provide more rapid\n                  feedback and keep the ball rolling; it's a lot easier to\n                    course correct every few days than once per week.</p>\n\n                  <p>I have yet to hit the upper bound on the number of\n                  undergrads in my lab, so if you're interested, please drop me\n                  a line.  I especially encourage sophomore and junior students\n                  to get involved (earlier is better), so that there's enough\n                  time to do the research and you get the benefit of being in a\n                  lab for longer periods.</p>\n\n                  <h4>Non-PhD External Students not at the University of Michigan</h4>\n\n                  <p>I unfortunately rarely work with undergraduate and masters\n                  students who are not physically at the University of Michigan.\n                  I still get emails from external students asking if we could\n                  together on something remotely and I really would love to, but\n                  my priority is to advise the current students at UM given the\n                  limited bandwith I have for advising.  Your best bet to work\n                  with me is to get admitted to one of our programs and then\n                    drop me an email.</p>\n\n                  <p><i>However</i>, I do on occasion have <u>self-funded</u>\n                  visiting students for the summer and (rarely) during the\n                  academic year.  In these cases, usually the student has some\n                  prior research experience, similar research interests, and\n                  (due to luck) there is a current project going on in my lab\n                  where they would be a good fit.  If you are a self-funded\n                  student who wants to come for the summer, you should send an\n                    email in February or March before the summer that\n                    </p><ol>\n                      <li> Describes your research experience and state what\n                      parts are relevant to the work going on in my group.\n                      </li><li> Clearly state why you want to work with me.  This\n                      lets me know that you are familiar with my work and know\n                        what you might be working on!\n                      </li><li> What you want to get out of a summer research\n                        experience and what you want to learn.  This helps me make\n                        sure that the trip is a success for you as well.\n                    </li></ol>\n                    I don't consider self-funded visiting students as \"free\n                    labor\" and strongly want to make sure that your stay is\n                    productive and a success for your career goals.\n                  <p></p>\n\n                  <p>If you're a <i>PhD student</i> somewhere else and want to\n                  work with me (while being external), this <i>could</i> happen\n                  under the right circumstances.  Typically, your advisor at\n                  your primary institution and I would co-advise you.  I\n                  typically only do these kinds of arrangements when I know your\n                  advisor (more common) or when the collaborative project make\n                  sense (rare).  To get this started, have your advisor email me\n                  (not you directly) about what the project is.</p>\n                  \n                  <h4>Postdocs</h4>\n\n                  <p>I would love to have you all in my lab but this is\n                  generally dependent on funding (but seriously, I would take\n                  you all if I could).  Email me if you think you're a good\n                  match and tell me why and we might be able to figure something\n                  out.  That said, at the moment, I'm not currently actively\n                  seeking postdocs (due to funding, of course).  If you're\n                  coming with your own funding, that changes everything, so drop\n                  me a line then.</p>\n                  \n                </div>\n              </section>\n\n\n\n              <section style=\"padding-top: 51px;\">\n                <p class=\"title\" data-section-title=\"\" style=\"left: 630px; height: 52px;\"><a href=\"#panel2\">FAQs</a></p>\n                <div class=\"content\" data-section-content=\"\">                  \n\n\n                  <p><b>Q: How do I pronounce your last name? </b><br>\n                    A: Like you would in the old country</p>\n                \n                  <p><b>Q: Which old country is that? </b><br>\n                    A: <span style=\"font-size: 20pt\"> \ud83e\udd37</span></p>\n\n                  <p><b>Q: Can I research with you or be a member of the Blablablab? </b><br>\n                    A: For an overly-detailed answer, click the \"Prospective students\" tab thingie above.
