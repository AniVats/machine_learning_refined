The Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs</h1>\n              \n        <!-- h1 before this file -->\n\n                  </div>\n      </div> <!-- /.section, /#top-content -->\n\n      <div class=\"container\">\n        <div id=\"content\">\n          <div class=\"column container\">\n              <div class=\"region region-content\">\n    <div id=\"block-system-main\" class=\"block block-system\">\n\n    \n  <div class=\"content\">\n\n  <!-- YOUR CONTENT HERE -->\n \n\n\n\n            <p><strong>Catalog Description:</strong> An introduction to programming and computer science focused on abstraction techniques as means to manage program complexity. Techniques include procedural abstraction; control abstraction using recursion, higher-order functions, generators, and streams; data abstraction using interfaces, objects, classes, and generic operators; and language abstraction using interpreters and macros. The course exposes students to programming paradigms, including functional, object-oriented, and declarative approaches. It includes an introduction to asymptotic analysis of algorithms. There are several significant programming projects.</p>\n    \n            <p><strong>Units:</strong> 4.0</p>\n    \n    \n    \n            <p><strong>Prerequisites:</strong> MATH 1A (may be taken concurrently); programming experience equivalent to that gained from a score of 3 or above on the Advanced Placement Computer Science A exam.</p>\n    \n            <p><strong>Credit Restrictions:</strong> Students will receive no credit for Computer Science 61A after completing Computer Science 47A or Computer Science 61AS. A deficient grade in Computer Science 61AS may be removed by taking Computer Science 61A.</p>\n    \n            <p><strong>Formats:</strong><br> Fall: 3.0 hours of lecture, 1.5 hours of discussion, and 1.5 hours of laboratory per week<br>\nSpring: 3.0 hours of lecture, 1.5 hours of discussion, and 1.5 hours of laboratory per week<br>\nSummer: 6.0 hours of lecture, 3.0 hours of discussion, and 3.0 hours of laboratory per week</p>\n    \n            <p><strong>Grading basis:</strong> letter</p>\n    \n            <p><strong>Final exam status:</strong> Written final exam conducted during the scheduled final exam period</p>\n    \n    \n    <hr>\n            <p><strong>Class Schedule (Spring 2020):</strong>\n            <br>MoWeFr 2:00PM - 2:59PM, Wheeler 150 \u2013\n            <a href=\"/Faculty/Homepages/denero.html\">John DeNero</a>        </p>\n    \n    \n    \n            <p><a href=\"http://inst.eecs.berkeley.edu/~cs61a\">Class homepage on inst.eecs</a></p>\n    \n            <p><a href=\"http://guide.berkeley.edu/search/?P=COMPSCI+61A\">General Catalog listing</a></p>\n    \n            <hr>\n        <p><strong>Department Notes:</strong> </p><p>\nWe follow the textbook <i>Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs</i> by Abelson and Sussman (second edition, MIT Press, 1996) fairly closely, but with somewhat more emphasis on symbolic computation and less on numerical examples from the calculus and number theory. A course outline follows.\n\n</p><ul><li>Functional abstraction\n<p>\nThis material comprises most of the first chapter of the text, and covers roughly three weeks of the course.\nIn the first week, we talk about the fundamentals of Lisp computation: names and values, evaluation, function definition and evaluation, and predicates.\nThe first week also includes an introduction to the UNIX operating system and the editor students will use to create programs. We introduce list processing procedures at the beginning (the text defers lists to chapter 2) to allow for symbolic examples. \nIn the second week, we discuss higher-order functions, including the use of functions as parameters,\nthe definition of functions with LAMBDA, and the use of functions that return functions as values. \nThe third week moves to recursion and covers the topic of processes and orders of growth, introducing the use of iterative algorithms for efficiency. Assignments provide practice in design and analysis via short exercises, and culminate in a rather longer programming problem that tries to tie everything together.\n</p>\n</li><li>Data abstraction\n<p>\nThis material appears in the second chapter of the text,\nand takes around three weeks to cover.\nWe start with the idea of data abstraction\nand techniques for implementing \"abstraction barriers.\"\nNext, we discuss the use of Scheme pairs\nto implement lists, trees, and other data structures.\nFinally, we spend a week on more advanced data abstraction techniques:\ntagged data, data-directed programming, and message-passing.\nSeveral assignments provide the students with practice in these areas,\nand the second course project combines the techniques taught here\nwith the procedure-as-data concepts covered earlier in the course.\n</p>\n</li><li>State and assignment\n<p>\nIn the next five weeks, we cover the use of state and local assignment\nto write efficient programs; this material comes from the third\nchapter of the text.\nThe first two weeks introduce the idea of object-oriented programming,\nassignment, and the environment\nmodel of evaluation that is needed to understand how local state is\nmaintained in Scheme.  In the third week we discuss mutable data.\nThe fourth week is about concurrency; the fifth week cover streams,\nan attempt to model time-varying state information within the\nfunctional programming approach.\nShort assignments during this section of the course are interspersed\nwith a substantial programming project using object-oriented techniques,\nsuch as an adventure game.\n</p>\n</li><li>Metalinguistic abstraction\n<p>\nAnother four-week period is devoted to the creation of new programming\nlanguages, as a still more powerful abstraction technique.  Two major\nexamples are presented: Lisp interpreters in the first two weeks and\na logic programming language in the next week.  Assignments include the\nlast major programming project, an interpreter for another programming\nlanguage.  This section of the course follows the fourth chapter of the text.\n</p>\n</li></ul><p></p>\n    \n            <p><strong>Related Areas:</strong></p>\n        <ul>\n        <li><a
