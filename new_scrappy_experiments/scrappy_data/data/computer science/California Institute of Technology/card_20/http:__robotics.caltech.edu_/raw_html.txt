As part of its contribution to the overall\nteam effort, Caltech is working on:\n</p>\n<ul><li> <b>Estimation:</b> Our goal is to fuse various visual modalities (stereo, LADAR, appearance) with</li></ul>\n<p>force-torque sensing, tactile sensing, and proprioception to better estimate the locations of the\nobjects to be manipulated as well as the posture of the arm.\n</p>\n<ul><li> <b>Grasp Planning:</b>  We are trying to extend the basic theory of <i>caging</i> manipulation to 3-dimensional objects.</li>\n<li> <b>Task Decomposition:</b>  We are investigating the use of formal systems theory (e.g., the use of correct-by-construction control synthesis based on Linear Temporal Logic system specification and model checking) to construct correct-by-design automata for complex manipulation tasks.</li></ul>\n</td>\n<td>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/index.php?title=Special:Upload&amp;wpDestFile=Robot_Drill.png\" class=\"new\" title=\"File:Robot Drill.png\">200px</a>\n</p>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<table width=\"100%\" border=\"1\" cellpadding=\"2\" cellspacing=\"2\">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td> <a href=\"/wiki/index.php?title=Special:Upload&amp;wpDestFile=DragonRunner.png\" class=\"new\" title=\"File:DragonRunner.png\">150px</a>\n</td>\n<td> <a href=\"/wiki/index.php?title=Special:Upload&amp;wpDestFile=RCTAsymbol.jpg\" class=\"new\" title=\"File:RCTAsymbol.jpg\">300px</a>\n</td>\n<td>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Robotic_Field_Manipulation.2FRCTA\">Robotic Field Manipulation/RCTA</span></h3>\n<p>We are part of the RCTA (Robotics Collaborative Technology Aliance) program, which is sponsored by\nthe Army Research Labs (ARL), and lead by General Dynamics Robotics Systmes (GDRS).  One of the main\nobjectives of this program is to develop the capabilities for mobile robots to carry out complex\noperations in unstructured field environments.  In collaboration with the Jet Propulsion Laboratory,\nwe are developing novel grasp planning algorithms for low-degree-of-freedom grippers, as well as\ntechniques to estimate the state of the grasped object and the manipulator system.\n</p>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<table width=\"100%\" border=\"1\" cellpadding=\"2\" cellspacing=\"2\">\n\n<tbody><tr valign=\"top\">\n<td>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Axel_and_DuAxel_Rovers_for_extreme_planetary_terrains\"><i>Axel</i> and <i>DuAxel</i> Rovers for extreme planetary terrains</span></h3>\n<p>Conventional robotic Martian explorers, such as Sojourner, Spirit, and Opportunity, have sufficient\nmobility to access ~60% of the Martian surface.  However, some of the most interesting science\ntargets occur in the currently inaccessible <i>extreme terrains,</i> such as steep craters, overhangs,\nloose soil, and layered stratigraphy.  Access to extreme terrains on other planets (besides Mars)\nand moons is also of potential interest.  In collaboration with JPL, we are developing the <i>Axel</i>\nand <i>DuAxel</i> rovers.  <i>Axel</i> is a minimalist tethered robot that can ascend and descend vertical\nand steeps slopes, as well as navigate over large (relative to the body size) obstacles.  In the\n<i>DuAxel</i> configuration, two Axels dock with a <i>central module</i> to form a self-contained\n4-wheeled rover, which can then disassemble as needed to allow one or both Axels to descend into\nextreme terrain. The goal of this work is to develop and demonstrate the motion planning, novel\nmobility mechanisms, mobility analysis, and steep terrain sampling technologies that would allow\nAxel and DuAxel to be viable concepts for future scientific missions to extreme terrains.\n</p>\n</td>\n<td>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/index.php?title=Special:Upload&amp;wpDestFile=DuAxel_Labels.jpg\" class=\"new\" title=\"File:DuAxel Labels.jpg\">400px</a>\n</p>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<table width=\"100%\" border=\"1\" cellpadding=\"2\" cellspacing=\"2\">\n\n<tbody><tr valign=\"top\">\n<td>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/index.php?title=Special:Upload&amp;wpDestFile=SteppingRobot.jpg\" class=\"new\" title=\"File:SteppingRobot.jpg\">260px</a>\n</p>\n</td>\n<td>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Locomotion_Rehabilitation_After_Severe_Spinal_Cord_Injury\">Locomotion Rehabilitation After Severe Spinal Cord Injury</span></h3>\n<p>Approximately 250,000 people in the U.S. suffer from a major Spinal Cord Injury (SCI), and ~11,000\nnew people will be afflicted each year.  Our lab collaborates with Prof. Reggie Edgerton at UCLA,\nProf. Susan Harkema at Univ. of Louisville, and Prof. Y.C. Tai here at Caltech to develop new\ntherapies and new technologies that hopefully one day will enable patients suffering from SCI to\npartially or fully recover the ability to walk.  Currently, we focus on these topics:\n</p>\n<ul><li> Novel <i>high density epidural spinal stimulating electrode arrays</i> for locomotion recovery.  For more on </li></ul>\n<p>this topic, <a rel=\"nofollow\" class=\"external text\" href=\"http://robotics.caltech.edu/~NIH_BRP/index.php/Main_Page\">see this link</a>.\n</p>\n<ul><li> New robotic mechanisms for active rehabilitation of SCI in animal (mice and rat) models.</li>\n<li> New adaptive training algorithms to optimize the rehabilitation afforded by robotic devices.</li>\n<li> Drug therapies to improve locomotion recovery.</li></ul>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<table width=\"100%\" border=\"1\" cellpadding=\"2\" cellspacing=\"2\">\n\n<tbody><tr valign=\"top\">\n<td>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Recent_Papers\">Recent Papers</span></h3>\n<p><b>Planning in Uncertain Environments</b>\n</p>\n<ul><li> Noel du Toit and Joel W. In collaboration with Prof. Hossein Hashimi at USC, we are investigating the use of UWB radar to detect and track human motion for safety and security applications.  \n</p>\n</td>\n<td>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/index.php?title=Special:Upload&amp;wpDestFile=OnePageOverview.jpg\" class=\"new\" title=\"File:OnePageOverview.jpg\">300px</a>\n</p>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<table width=\"100%\" border=\"1\" cellpadding=\"2\" cellspacing=\"2\">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/index.php?title=Special:Upload&amp;wpDestFile=Worm03animated_web.gif\" class=\"new\" title=\"File:Worm03animated web.gif\">Nematode</a>\n</p>\n</td>\n<td>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Animal_Tracking_and_Activity_Recognition\">Animal Tracking and Activity Recognition</span></h3>\n<p>We are interested in developing methods to automatically identify and classify \"activities\" in data\nstreams, such as video sequences. A practical application and motivation for this work is automated\ntracking and recognition of biological organism behavior in controlled laboratory environments.  \n</p>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<table width=\"100%\" border=\"1\" cellpadding=\"2\" cellspacing=\"2\">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Sensor-Based_Motion_Planning_and_Sensor_Processing\">Sensor-Based Motion Planning and Sensor Processing</span></h3>\n<p><i>Sensor Based Planning</i> incorporates sensor information, reflecting the current state of the\nenvironment, into a robot\\'s planning process, as opposed to classical planning , where full\nknowledge of the world\\'s geometry is assumed to be known prior to the planning event. Current and\nrecent interests of our group include\n</p>\n<ul><li> Motion planning in Cluttered, Dynamic, and Uncertain environments</li>\n<li> Sensor-based motion planning algorithms</li></ul>\n</td>\n<td>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/index.php?title=Special:Upload&amp;wpDestFile=Rocky7.gif\" class=\"new\" title=\"File:Rocky7.gif\">240px</a>\n</p>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<table width=\"100%\" border=\"1\" cellpadding=\"2\" cellspacing=\"2\">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/index.php?title=Special:Upload&amp;wpDestFile=MovableProbe1.gif\" class=\"new\" title=\"File:MovableProbe1.gif\">File:MovableProbe1.gif</a>\n</p>\n</td>\n<td>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Neural_Prosthetics_and_Brain-Machine_Interfaces\">Neural Prosthetics and Brain-Machine Interfaces</span></h3>\n<p>A neural prosthesis is a <i>direct brain interface</i> that enables a human, via the use of surgically \nimplanted electrode arrays and associated computer decoding algorithms, to control external \nelectromechanical devices by pure thought alone.  In this manner, some useful motor functions that \nhave been lost through disease or accident can be partially restored.  Our lab collaborates with the \nlaboratories of Prof. Richard Andersen and Prof. Y.C. Tai to develop neural prostheses and brain-machine \ninterfaces.  Our group focuses on these particular issues:\n</p>\n<ul><li> <b>Autonomously Positioned (robotic) Neural Recording Electrodes.</b> To optimize the quality of</li></ul>\n<p>the neural signal recorded by an extracellular electrode, the active recording site must be\npositioned very close (at least within 30 microns, and preferably a few microns from the soma) to\nthe neural cell body.  However, due to blood pressure variations, breathing, and mechanical shocks,\nthe electrode-soma geometry varies significantly over time.  We have developed algorithms which\nallow an actuated electrode to autonomously reposition itself in real time to maintain high quality\nneural recordings.\n</p>\n<ul><li> <b>Neural decoding algorithms.</b> A <i>decoding</i> algorithm attempts to decode, or decipher, the</li></ul>\n<p>intent of a paralyzed neural prosthetic user from the recorded electrode signals.  Neural decoding\nhas become a well developed subject.  We have chosen to explore the concept of a <i>supervisory</i>\ndecoder<i> whose aim is to estimate the current cognitive and planning state of the prosthetic user.</i>\nE.g., is the user awake? Do they want to use the prosthetic?  Are they currently in the planning\nprocess?  Do they want to execute the plan?  Do the want to change or scrub the current prosthetic\naction?  We have chosen to formulate the design of a supervisory decoder as a problem in hybrid\nsystem identification.\n</p>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n</td>\n</tr>\n</tbody></table>\n\n<!-- \nNewPP limit
