class=\"MsoNormal\" style=\"margin-top:.25in\">I lead a research group on <a href=\"http://web.cse.ohio-state.edu/siefast/group\">Dependable Distributed and\n  Networked Systems</a><b>.&nbsp; </b>The group works on the foundations of\n  fault-tolerance, security, and timeliness properties,&nbsp;&nbsp; develops\n  design, verification, and implementation methods,&nbsp;&nbsp; and builds\n  prototypes of dependable systems for new application areas.&nbsp; </p>\n  <p style=\"margin-top:.25in\">We are especially interested in discovering new\n  methods for scalable dependability,<span class=\"GramE\">&nbsp; as</span> well as\n  in characterizing the differences between reasoning about system correctness\n  versus reasoning about systems dependability. Our methods draw from the\n  theory of self-stabilization, and exploit formal specifications of and\n  \"white box\" knowledge about the system. Demonstrations of our work\n  are presently focused on embedded sensor network applications and internet\n  services. <br>\n  <br>\n  A notable recent accomplishment in wireless sensor networks is <span style=\"mso-bidi-font-size:13.5pt;mso-bidi-font-weight:bold\"><a href=\"http://cast.cse.ohio-state.edu/exscal/index.php?page=main\"><span class=\"SpellE\">ExScal</span></a></span> which demonstrated a perimeter security\n  application with over 1000 sensor nodes and over 200 802.11b backbone nodes\n  spread over a 1.3km by 300m area. As of December 2004, <span class=\"SpellE\">ExScal</span>\n  was the largest wireless sensor network deployed and likely also the largest\n  802.11b peer-to-peer ad hoc network deployed. The research led to the\n  development of <a href=\"http://kansei.cse.ohio-state.edu/KanseiGenie/\">Kansei</a>,\n  which is an interactive, heterogeneous, large-scale wireless sensor network\n  environment for testing and development. The stationary array of Kansei is\n  housed in a warehouse near our Campus and presently contains over 700 sensor\n  nodes supporting different hardware and software platforms. Other Kansei\n  arrays are located in our department building, and supporting sensing for our\n  in-building mobile peer-to-peer <a href=\"http://kansei.netlab.ohio-state.edu/peoplenet/index.php\">People Net</a>,\n  and being cloned nationwide as part of our effort to integrate Kansei with\n  GENI experimentation infrastructure.</p>\n  <p style=\"margin-top:.25in\">In the last few years, we have collaborated\n  actively with colleagues at UT at Austin, Iowa, Michigan State, Kent State,\n  UC Berkeley, and MIT, as well as other university and industry partners\n  (research resulting from our DARPA NEST project may be found in our <a href=\"http://nest.netlab.ohio-state.edu/\">Stabilization in NEST</a> page) and\n  with members of the Systems and Networking Group at Microsoft Research in Redmond,\n  WA&nbsp; (research resulting from the MSR Aladdin Home Networking Project may\n  be found in the <a href=\"http://web.cse.ohio-state.edu/siefast/group/publications.html\">Publications</a>\n  page). In 2003, we completed a smart-dust sensor network field experiment on <a href=\"http://web.cse.ohio-state.edu/siefast/nest/nest_webpage/ALineInTheSand.html\">A\n  Line in the Sand</a> for DARPA and a demonstration of <a href=\"http://web.cse.ohio-state.edu/siefast/msr-cont-self-maint/\">continuous\n  self-maintenance</a> at the Microsoft Research Faculty Summit.
